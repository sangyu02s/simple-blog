Among the poems often quoted in English classrooms, Robert Frost's *The Road Not Taken* remains especially memorable because it sounds simple while carrying a deep emotional echo. The poem begins with a traveler standing before two roads in a yellow wood, unable to take both.

What makes the poem enduring is not only the image of choice, but the calm uncertainty beneath it. Frost does not present the speaker as heroic or entirely sure. Instead, the poem gently admits that life is often understood backward. We choose, we move on, and only years later do we begin to tell ourselves a story about why that choice mattered.

This is why the poem still resonates today. It speaks not just about decision, but about memory, interpretation, and the human habit of giving meaning to the paths we have already walked.
